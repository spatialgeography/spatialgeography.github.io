# Part 3: Planning Region

## Meaning
> A planning region is an areal unit considered fit for regional planning and distinctive in itself while remaining part of the larger spatial whole.

## Definitions

| Scholar | Definition |
|:---|:---|
| **Keeble** | A planning region should be large enough for substantial changes in population and employment within it, yet small enough for problems to be viewed as a whole |
| **Klassen** | Must be large enough for investment decisions, supply labour to its own industries, have homogeneous economic structure, at least one growth point, and common awareness of problems |
| **John Friedmann** | Regional planning is the process of formulating and classifying social objectives in the ordering of activities in supra-urban space |
| **Harvey S. Perloff** | Regional planning does not take place on the head of a pin |

## Characteristics of a Good Planning Region
- Adequate size depending on purpose
- Clear boundaries
- Functional unity
- Natural cohesion
- Economic viability
- Social harmony
- Similarity of problems
- Regional consciousness
- Administrative convenience

## Types by Scale
- **Micro planning** — smallest planning region
- **Meso planning** — combination of at least two micro regions
- **Macro planning** — large region with broad planning scope
- **Damodar Valley Basin** in India is an example of **meso planning**

# Part 4: Philosophical Base of Regional Planning

## Origin
- Regional planning derives much of its philosophy from **Geography**
- Geography is recognised as an **environmental science**
- Philosophy is based on the **totality of space** and unity of space-content
- Also based on **horizontal unity of space** and spatial interaction

## Practical Help from Geography to Regional Planning
1. Providing geographical database
2. Demarcation of regional boundaries
3. Conduct of regional surveys
4. Preparation of maps and diagrams
5. Understanding spatial interaction processes

## Patrick Geddes' Trinity
- **Place** — physical environment
- **Folk** — people and demographic characteristics
- **Work** — economic activities and livelihood

# Part 5: Benton MacKaye and Planning Thought

## Founder Role
- **Benton MacKaye (1928)** — regarded as founder of Regional Planning
- Also associated with **New Exploration: A Philosophy of Regional Planning**

## Famous Statement
> *"Every deliberate action must start in the mind, the realm of thought. It must be conceived and rehearsed in the realm of thought before it can take place in the physical."*

## Four Pillars of Planning
1. **Perception** — perceiving the order to be created
2. **Revelation** — revealing potentialities and limitations of nature
3. **Preparation** — preparing the physical schema or plan
4. **Execution** — implementing strategy and objectives

## Chief Objective
- Cultivation of **habitability in all regions of human occupancy**

## Twin Objectives of Regional Planning
1. **Cultivating habitability**
2. **Community building**

# Part 6: Need and Principles of Regional Planning

## Need for Regional Planning
- To channelise world development in the right direction
- To resolve critical contemporary problems
- Due to emergency conditions
- Due to rapid population increase
- Because administrative boundaries often do not match functional regions
- Because of rapid transport and communication development
- Because regional planning has clear spatial significance

## Seven Principles of Regional Development
1. Principle of vertical unity of phenomena
2. Horizontal spatial unity
3. Space-time continuum
4. Comprehensive development
5. Community development
6. Equilibrium between social desirability and economic viability
7. Ecological equilibrium

## Sustainable Development Parameters
1. Maximum growth without jeopardising people and resources
2. Maintaining ecological processes
3. Sustaining resource potential
4. Maintaining genetic diversity
5. Giving equal status to economic quantity and quality

## Triple Bottom Line
- **John Elkington** — environment, society and economy

# Part 10: Regional Development

## Meaning
- Regional growth means increase in income / per capita income

## Perspectives of Development
- **Economic perspective** — growth, standard of living
- **Sociological perspective** — development of people, institutions and social structure

## Factors Controlling Development
1. Physical resources
2. Degree of technological advancement
3. Social structure
4. Economic setup
5. Polity

## Positive / Negative Correlations
- Positive correlation between development and technological advancement
- Negative correlation between per capita GNP and dictatorial tendencies

## Basic Concepts of Regional Development
1. Identification and analysis of regional disparities
2. Analysis of spatial diffusion process
3. Analysis of spatial consequences of development, especially ecological balance
4. Development of spatial models of development

## Measures of Regional Disparity
1. Summation of ranks
2. Quartile index
3. Aggregation of relative score / resource
4. Rank categorisation
5. Deprivation index
6. Principal component analysis

## Quantile Methods
- Quartiles = 4
- Deciles = 10
- Octiles = 8
- Sextiles = 6
- **Asok Mitra** used the **octiles technique**

## Z-Score Method
- Score of each areal unit divided by the regional average to obtain **Z-score** for each indicator

## Principal Component Analysis
- Reduces size of variable matrix to a comprehensible level
- Number of components equals number of variables, though some are redundant

# Part 12: Regional Imbalance

## Main Thrust
- Main thrust of regional planning is to **remove areal disparity**

## Classical Economist View
- Labour would flow from high-wage region to low-wage region

## New Method
- **Raghu Ranjan** suggested a new method for measuring regional imbalance

## Problem Regions
- Show little promise of development in near future

## Dynamic Regions
- Support advanced industrial areas and predominantly urban population

## Perspective Regions
- Have immense resource potential but face socio-economic obstacles to technological transformation

## Major Regions
- Regions with minimum disparities within and distinctiveness from neighbours regarding character and development resources

## Axial Development Region
- Develops along transportation lines or irrigation channels

- Convergence hypothesis linked to **Ohlin** and **Samuelson** in interregional context

- **Douglas C. North**
- Emphasises role of **exogenous** forces in regional growth
- Regional growth model linked with externally generated demand

# Part 16: Myrdal's Cumulative Causation Theory

## Core Idea
- *"An area is poor because it is poor"*
- Development tends to be cumulative and self-reinforcing

## Main Concepts
- **Spread effect**
- **Backwash effect**

### Spread Effect
- Advantages spread to surrounding areas
- Industries attract inflow of labour, capital and investment

### Backwash Effect
- Outflow of labour and capital from poorer region to richer region
- Produces stagnation or regression in lagging areas

## Book
- **Economic Theory and Underdeveloped Regions**

## Kaldor
- Efficiency wages = Monetary wages / Labour productivity

## Dixon & Thirlwall
- **Verdoorn effect** — growth and labour productivity depend on output growth

---

# Part 17: Friedmann's Core-Periphery Model

## Main Idea
- Spatial organisation and national development are closely related

## Structure
- **Core** — rich in capital, labour scarcity
- **Periphery** — rich in raw materials and labour

## Four Stages of Development
1. Agricultural society; no major flow of capital
2. Movement of raw material to the core; spread and backwash effects begin
3. Development of regional centres; trickle down and backward effects
4. Wider diffusion of development to multiple centres

# Part 18: Growth Pole Theory

## Francois Perroux
- Growth appears **not everywhere at the same time**
- Space is treated as a **field of forces**
- Development starts from dynamic, propulsive industries

## Boudeville
- Gave geographical expression to Perroux's abstract theory
- Defined growth pole as a **set of expanding industries located in an urban area including further development of economic activity through its zone of influence**
- Weighted index number method associated with Boudeville

## Hirschman
- **Polarisation** and **Trickle Down** effects

## Growth Foci / Growth Centres
- **V.L.S. Prakasa Rao & R.P. Mishra**
- **Growth Pole and Growth Center for Regional Economic Development in India** — Mishra, Rao and Sundaram
