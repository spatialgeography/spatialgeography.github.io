## Atmospheric Circulation



The unequal heating of the Earth’s surface creates pressure gradients, giving rise to complex global and regional wind systems that transport heat and moisture across the globe.

### Wind Systems & Pressure Belts
* **General Circulation (Tri-Cellular Model)**: The Hadley Cell drives trade winds near the equator; the Ferrel Cell drives westerlies in the mid-latitudes; and the Polar Cell creates polar easterlies. This three-cell structure explains the primary latitudinal shifting of climate zones.
* **Pressure Belts**: Include the Equatorial Low (Doldrums), Subtropical High (Horse Latitudes), Subpolar Low, and Polar High. Their seasonal migration, following the apparent movement of the sun, dictates wet and dry seasons across the tropics.
* **Jet Streams**: Narrow, high-speed meandering air currents in the upper troposphere. The Subtropical and Polar jet streams significantly influence the paths of surface weather systems and aviation routes.
* **Monsoons**: Massive seasonal reversals of wind direction caused by the differential heating of land and sea. The Indian Monsoon is the most prominent, fundamentally shaping the hydrology, agriculture, and economy of South Asia.
* **Local Winds**: Thermally driven local circulations including land/sea breezes, mountain/valley anabatic and katabatic winds, and regional phenomena like the warm/dry Chinook (Rockies), Foehn (Alps), and the cold Mistral (France).
