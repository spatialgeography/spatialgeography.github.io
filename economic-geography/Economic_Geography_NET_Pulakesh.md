# Part 1: Agricultural Geography — Whittlesey's Classification (1936)

> **Derwent Whittlesey (1936)** — *"Major Agricultural Regions of the Earth"* (AAG, 1938)

> *"Physical environment sets the limit for production of crops and raising of livestock"*

## 5-Fold Criteria for Agricultural Classification
1. Crops and livestock combination
2. Methods and techniques used to grow crops and raise livestock
3. Intensiveness in the application of inputs (labour, capital, etc.)
4. Livestock purpose — subsistence or commercial
5. Ensemble of structures used to house farm buildings

### 3. Rudimentary Sedentary Tillage
- To feed themselves and family
- Practised where environment is not suitable for commercial production
- **Distribution:** Tropical lands — Central & South America, Africa, SE Asia
- Main crops: Potatoes, maize, sorghum, cassava, banana
- Sedentary or semi-shifting cultivation

### 4. Intensive Subsistence Tillage
- Confined to **monsoon lands of Asia** — China, Japan, Bangladesh, India, Thailand, Sri Lanka, Malaysia, Indonesia
- Highly populated; greater intensity of land tillage
- Virtually a **monoculture** — dominated by wet paddy cultivation

### 5. Subsistence Crop and Livestock Farming
- Farmers produce for own consumption; no cash income
- Cash crops sold in local urban markets; capital generation very low
- **Main crops:** Barley and wheat

### 6. Mediterranean Agriculture
- Most popular and stable type of agriculture
- More stable collaboration between man and nature
- **Areas:** Mediterranean Sea borders — Southern Europe, North Africa, Western Asia

- Commercial grazing over extensive area; semi-arid and arid regions

### 8. Extensive Commercial Grain Farming
- Commercial grain farming; mechanised and technology-heavy
- **Farm size:** 240 to 1600 hectares

### 9. Commercial Livestock and Crop Farming
- Stemmed from medieval agriculture of NW Europe
- **Farm size:** 240 to 1600 hectares; highly mechanised
- Main products: Corn, soybean, mink

### 13. Commercial Plantation Farming

- Large-scale, capitalised, centralised system
- **Main crops:** Cotton, sugarcane, coffee, tea, cocoa, rubber and tobacco

# Part 2: Agricultural Productivity

> **Agricultural Productivity** = total agricultural output per unit of cultivated area per agricultural worker or per unit of input (in monetary values)

## Types of Productivity

### Land Productivity
- More efficient use of factors of production: environment, arable land, labour, capital
- Ability to exploit abiotic resources for agricultural production

### Three Main Indicators of Agricultural Productivity
1. Yield
2. Grain equivalents
3. Cropping system

## Measurement Techniques

| Technique | Scholar | Year | Method |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| 1 | Khusno | 1964 | Paid out cost in relation to output as a measure of farm efficiency |
| 2 | Buck | 1967 | Production in terms of grain equivalents per head of population |
| 3 | E. de Vries | — | All output expressed in terms of mid milled-rice equivalents (Asian countries) |
| 4 | Clark & Haswell | — | Wheat equivalents per person |
| 5 | Kendall | 1939 | Output per unit area (yield per hectare); 48 administrative towns of England |
| 6 | Sapre & Deshpande | 1964 | Weightage to ranking order of output per unit area with % share under each crop |
| 7 | Bhatia | 1967 | Two assumptions: (1) all physical & human factors (2) cropped land among crops reflects land utilisation; Iya = Yc/Yr × 100 |
| 8 | L.D. Stamp | 1967 | Carrying capacity of land in terms of population; 'standard nutrition unit' and 'caloric value' |
| 9 | Enyedi & Shafi | 1964 / 1972–74 | Productivity Index = (Y/Yn) ÷ (T/Tn); 12 food crops in India |
| 10 | Jasbir Singh | 1972–74 | Modification of Stamp's technique; carrying capacity as % for entire region: Cp = Co/Sn |
| 11 | Jasbir Singh | 1976 | Crop yield and concentration indices ranking coefficient: Yi = Yac/Yar × 100 |
| 12 | Hussain | 1976 | Agricultural productivity of Sutlej–Ganga plains |
| 13 | V.R. Singh | 1979 | 12th technique; delimiting agricultural productivity using intensity and spread indices of yield, grain equivalents and cropping pattern |

## Cropping Intensity

> Agricultural productivity can be increased by: (1) Expanding cropped area OR (2) Increasing intensity of cropping

**Formula:**

> **Cropping Intensity = (Total Cropped Area / Net Sown Area) × 100**

**Example:** Rabi = 700 ha, Kharif = 500 ha, Net Sown Area = 1000 ha
→ (700 + 500) / 1000 × 100 = **120%**

# Part 3: Crop Combination

> Number and diversity of crops grown in a particular area during a specific time interval
> **Weaver (1954)** — advantage of crop combination:
- Adequate understanding of geographical crops
- For composite realities guiding distribution analysis
- Complex structure for valid agricultural regions
- Comprehensive basis for regional planning

## Contributing Scholars
- Weaver (1954)
- Jasbir Singh (1971)
- Singh and Dhillon (1984)
- Tinath and Krishan (1966)

## Method 1: Arbitrary Choice Method
- First 2–3 crops are shown and remaining excluded
- Not sensitive; fear of repetition; increases complexity with more crops

## Method 2: Statistical Method — J.C. Weaver (1954)
- Studied complex crop combinations in **Middle West (USA)**
- Compared **1081 counties**

> **Variance = Σd² / N; Standard Deviation = √(Σd²/n)**
> d = difference between actual and percentage crop area; n = number of crops

### Drawbacks of Weaver's Method
- Crops covering insignificant area may be of great regional importance
- Tends to show lowest deviation for a combination
- Unwieldy for areas of high crop diversification
- Involves laborious calculations

## Modifications of Weaver's Method

| Scholar | Year | Modification |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Doi** | 1959 | Substituted Weaver's variance (Σd²/n) with sum of square deviation (Σd²) |
| **Peter Scott** | 1963 | Animals are as important as crops in determining crop combination (Tasmania) |
| **Thomas** | — | Modified variance to minimum variance / least standard deviation |
| **J.T. Coppock** | 1964 | Modified Scott's method; converted all animals to common unit based on food quality |
| **Singh** | 1974 | Further modification of Weaver's technique |

# Part 5: Transport Models

> Models are classified on 3 bases:
> 1. Transport Corridor (Location & Accessibility)
> 2. Spatial Interaction
> 3. Spatial Input–Output

## 1. Location & Accessibility Models
- A modification of the **Central Place Model**

## 2. Spatial Interdependence Model
- **1961** — by Gotman (Goldman)
- Specialisation and interdependency taken into account
- *"Specialisation occurs at low cost and tries to increase trade"*
- Transport basis of regional analysis

## 3. Distribution / Flow Model
- Large urban centres are collection points for smaller centres
- Human behaviour tries to visit particular place at particular time

## 4. Lowry Model — 1964
- **First model of transport and land use**
- Basically demand-driven; flow-chart form
- Spatial interaction of basic employment affects zone of transparency

### Lowry Model Flowchart
```
Start with export industry employment levels
        ↓
Use gravity model to allocate export industry workers to residences
        ↓
Estimate levels of local sector demand
        ↓
Allocate local service industry workers to residences
        ↓
Re-estimate local service demand
        ↓
Check if last step changed demand & constraints are violated
        ↓
If yes → repeat local service allocation
If no → solution is completed
```

## 5. ITLUP — Integrated Transport Land Use Package
- By **Putman**
- Three components:
  - **DRAM** — Disaggregated Residential Allocation Model
  - **EAM** — Employment Allocation Model
  - **TDM** — Travel Demand Model
- Covers residential, employment and travel needs

## 6. LILT — Least Integrated Land Use Transport Model
- By **Machett**
- Combines concepts of Lowry + Carowhender

## 7. IRPUD Model
- Macro, Meso and Micro scale concept
- Explains inter-regional pre-location
- Services, new job or job change

## 8. Cellular Automata
- Explains complex behaviour of social and natural processes
- Considers: vegetation, hydrology, soil, etc.
- **5 Basic Characteristics:**
  1. Talks about discrete sites
  2. Evolution of discrete sites
  3. Each site has a finite value
  4. Deterministic Rules
  5. Rules dependent on neighbourhood

## 9. MEPLAN Model
- Interaction between Market & Land
- Aggregate model — market and transport facilities
- Divides space into zones; finds interaction between market and transport/land networks

## 10. TRANVS Model
- Simulates land use, transport, economic activity and financial activity in a region
- Demand-driven model; production cost between origin and destination

## 11. PECAS Model
- **Production, Exchange, Consumption, Allocation System**
- **Three levels:**
  - Level 1: Activity Allocation
  - Level 2: Technical Options
  - Level 3: Exchange Options

## 12. DELTA Model
- For both **Urban and Regional Level**
- Input to land use model
- Explains demography and employment exchange
- Economic growth model; predicts location

# Part 9: Weber's Theory of Industrial Location

> **Alfred Weber (1909)** — *Theory of the Location of Industries*

- Location of industry determined by **minimum transport cost**
- Three factors:
  1. **Transport costs** (primary factor)
  2. **Labour costs** (secondary factor)
  3. **Agglomeration/Deglomeration** (tertiary factor)

## Weber's Material Index (MI)

> **MI = Weight of localised materials / Weight of finished product**

- MI > 1 → industry locates near **raw material** (material oriented)
- MI < 1 → industry locates near **market** (market oriented)
- MI = 1 → industry locates anywhere (footloose)

## Isodapane
- Line joining points of **equal transport cost** around the least cost location
- **Critical Isodapane** — isodapane where saving in labour = extra transport cost

## Lösch's Theory
- **August Lösch (1954)** — *"The Economics of Location"*
- Maximum **profit** orientation (unlike Weber's minimum cost)
- Hexagonal market areas for optimal spatial distribution
- **Landscape of hexagonal nets** = Löschian Landscape

# Part 10: Van Thünen's Model

> **Johann Heinrich von Thünen (1826)** — *Der Isolierte Staat* (The Isolated State)

- First model of agricultural land use
- Single central city surrounded by uniform plain with equal fertile soil
- **Distance from market** determines what is grown where

## Concentric Zones (from city outward)

| Zone | Land Use | Reason |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Zone 1 | Horticulture & Dairy | Highly perishable; must be close |
| Zone 2 | Forestry (firewood/timber) | Heavy; high transport cost |
| Zone 3 | Crop Farming (Intensive) | Labour intensive; moderate distance |
| Zone 4 | Crop Farming (Extensive) | Less intensive; farther from city |
| Zone 5 | Livestock Ranching | Extensive; least perishable |
| Zone 6 | Wilderness | No economic use |

## Assumptions
- Uniform fertile soil and climate
- Single central market
- Uniform transport cost in all directions
- Isolated state (no external markets)
- Farmers seek maximum profit

## Modifications
- Two cities → zones distort
- River → zones extend along navigable route
- Second smaller city → creates secondary pattern

# Part 11: Classification of Industries

## Primary Industry
- Directly based on natural resources
- Agriculture, forestry, fishing, mining, quarrying

## Secondary Industry
- Manufacturing and processing
- Light and heavy manufacturing

## Tertiary Industry
- Service sector
- Trade, transport, communication, banking, education, health

## Quaternary Industry
- Knowledge-based; information services
- Research & Development, IT, consulting

## Resource-Based Industries
- Located near raw material source
- E.g., Sugar mills, paper mills, iron & steel, cement

## Footloose Industries
- Not tied to any specific location
- E.g., Electronics, IT, watches, jewellery
- Depend on skilled labour and accessibility
- Located where best combination of factors available

# Part 13: Sustainable Development

> **Brundtland Commission (1987):** *"Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs"*

## Triple Bottom Line (3 Ps)
- **People** (Social sustainability)
- **Planet** (Environmental sustainability)
- **Profit** (Economic sustainability)

## Renewable Resources
- Can be replenished naturally
- Examples: Solar energy, wind, water (hydro), biomass, tidal energy

## Non-Renewable Resources
- Finite; cannot be replenished within human timescales
- Examples: Coal, petroleum, natural gas, minerals, nuclear fuels

# Part 14: Accessibility and Connectivity

## Key Concepts
- **Accessibility** — ease with which any place can be reached from other places
- **Connectivity** — number of direct links between nodes in a network

## Network Analysis Measures

| Measure | Formula | Description |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Beta Index (β)** | β = e/v | Edges (e) / Vertices (v); degree of connectivity |
| **Gamma Index (γ)** | γ = e / 3(v−2) | Actual edges / Maximum possible edges |
| **Alpha Index (α)** | α = (e−v+1) / (2v−5) | Ratio of circuits to maximum circuits |
| **Eta Index (η)** | η = L/e | Total network length (L) / number of edges |
| **Cyclomatic Number (μ)** | μ = e−v+G | Number of independent circuits |
| **Pi Index (π)** | π = L/d | Total length / diameter of network |

- **Detour Index** — degree to which actual route exceeds straight-line distance

# Part 15: Comparative Cost Advantage

> **David Ricardo** — Theory of **Comparative Advantage**
> A country should specialise in producing goods in which it has lower *relative* cost

## Heckscher–Ohlin Theory
- Countries export goods that use their abundant factors intensively
- Countries import goods that use their scarce factors intensively

## Inter-Regional vs. Intra-Regional Trade

| Type | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Inter-regional** | Trade between different regions / countries |
| **Intra-regional** | Trade within a region or country |
