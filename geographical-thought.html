Geographical Thought

Evolution of geographic ideas from ancient civilizations to contemporary paradigms.

Welcome to the Geographical Thought module of Geography OpenCourseWare. This course traces the intellectual history of geography as a discipline — from the earliest map-makers and travelers to modern critical and quantitative paradigms.

Geographic Knowledge During the Ancient and Medieval Periods

The foundations of geography as a field of study were laid by ancient civilizations. Their contributions ranged from astronomical observations and mathematical calculations to descriptive accounts of people and places.

Ancient Greek Scholars

The Greeks were pioneers in mathematical and descriptive geography. - Homer: The father of geography; author of Iliad and Odyssey. - Thales of Miletus: Proposed the first theorems of geometry; believed Earth to be a disk floating in water. - Anaximander: Credited with drawing the first map of the known world to scale; used the Gnomon. - Hecataeus: The father of prose; wrote Ges-periodos (Description of the Earth). - Herodotus: Known for his historical and ethnographic geoinformation; “All history must be treated geographically.” - Aristotle: Laid the foundations of mathematical geography; argued for the sphericity of the Earth. - Eratosthenes: Calculated the Earth’s circumference almost exactly; termed the word “Geography.” - Hipparchus: Divided the circle into 360 degrees; developed the concept of latitude and longitude.

Ancient Roman Scholars

  • Strabo: Compiled the 17-volume Geographica, providing a comprehensive account of the Roman world.
  • Claudius Ptolemy: Created the Geographica atlas; developed map projections (conic and stereographic).

Ancient Indian Scholars

  • Varahamihira: Discussed the shape of the Earth and its rotation in Surya Siddhanta.
  • Brahmagupta: Contributed significantly to mathematical geography and base mathematics for navigation.
  • Aryabhatta: Proposed that the Earth rotates on its axis and orbits the Sun.
  • Bhaskaracharya: Discussed gravity and spherical trigonometry in Siddhanta Shiromani.

Ancient Chinese Scholars

  • Zhang Heng: Invented the world’s first seismoscope.
  • Chang Chien: Known for his explorations and reports on Central Asia (Silk Road).
  • Huan Tsang (Xuanzang): Detailed accounts of geography and culture in South Asia/India.
  • Zheng He: Great explorer who conducted seven voyages covering the Indian Ocean.

Medieval Arab Geographers

Arab scholars preserved and expanded upon Greek and Roman knowledge during the “Dark Ages” in Europe. - Al-Battani: Improved calculations of the length of the year and planetary motion. - Al-Masudi: The “Herodotus of the Arabs”; detailed descriptions of climates and cultures. - Ibn Sina (Avicenna): Contributed to geomorphology and climatology. - Al-Idrisi: Created the Tabula Rogeriana, one of the most advanced world maps of the Middle Ages. - Ibn Battuta: Traveled over 75,000 miles; wrote Rihla (The Travels). - Ibn Khaldun: A pioneer of sociology and political geography; author of Muqaddimah. - Al-Biruni: Expert in geodesy; calculated the Earth’s radius and geographic coordinates.

Foundations of Modern Geography

The transition from descriptive classical geography to the modern scientific discipline was marked by the era of exploration and the philosophical contributions of European thinkers.

The Age of Discovery and Transition

Pioneers who advanced cartography and regional description during the 16th to 18th centuries: - Gerardus Mercator: Developed the Mercator projection, revolutionizing navigation. - Sebastian Münster: Author of Cosmographia, the earliest German description of the world. - Peter Apian: Astronomer and cartographer; worked on mathematical geography. - Cluverius (Philipp Clüver): The founder of historical geography. - Waldseemüller: Created the first map to use the name “America.” - Abraham Ortelius: Created the first modern atlas, Theatrum Orbis Terrarum. - Bernhard Varenius: Distinguished between “General” (Universal) and “Special” (Regional) geography in his work Geographica Generalis. - Immanuel Kant: Proposed the “Exceptionalism” of geography; viewed geography as a “chorological” science (study of space).

The Founders of Modern Geography

The early 19th century saw geography established as a rigorous academic field through two major German scholars:

  1. Alexander von Humboldt (1769–1859)
    • Author of the 5-volume Cosmos.
    • Emphasized systematic, empirical research and the Unity of Nature.
    • Introduced isotherms and studied the relationship between elevation and vegetation.
  2. Carl Ritter (1779–1859)
    • Author of the 19-volume Die Erdkunde.
    • Emphasized regional geography (Länderkunde) and the teleological view (Earth designed for humans).
    • Focused on the relationship between man and land.

Historical Timeline Overview

  1. Ancient Period: Focused on mathematical foundations, exploration, and descriptive regional accounts (Greeks and Romans).
  2. Middle Ages (Arab Period): Preserved classical knowledge and advanced mapping/travel writing.
  3. Renaissance/Transition: Great Age of Discovery; invention of sophisticated map projections (Mercator).
  4. Modern Period: Institutionalization of Geography as a science; development of schools of thought (German, French, etc.).
  5. Post-Modern Period: Shift towards critical theories, quantitative methods, and diverse perspectives (Humanism, Radicalism).
