## Types of Farming


Different parts of the world have cultivated distinct farming systems adapted to their specific environmental constraints and economic demands.

### Farming Systems
* **Shifting Cultivation**: Also known as slash-and-burn, this is the oldest form of agriculture (e.g., Jhum in India, Milpa in Central America). Farmers clear forest patches, burn the debris for nutrient-rich ash, and move to a new plot when soil fertility declines.
* **Pastoral Nomadism**: A subsistence strategy based on the herding of domesticated animals over expansive dryland territories, such as the Sahel or Central Asian steppes, dictated by the seasonal availability of pasture and water.
* **Intensive Subsistence (Wet Rice)**: Dominant in densely populated Monsoon Asia. It is highly labor-intensive, relying on meticulous water management and terracing to maximize the yield of small landholdings.
* **Plantation Agriculture**: A legacy of colonialism, involving large-scale commercial monoculture of cash crops like tea, coffee, rubber, sugarcane, and palm oil, primarily located in the tropics but owned by multinational corporations.
* **Commercial Grain Farming**: Highly mechanized, extensive farming of single cereal crops (wheat, corn) on vast plains, such as the North American Prairies, the Pampas of Argentina, and the Steppes of Russia.
* **Mediterranean Agriculture**: Highly specialized farming occurring along the Mediterranean Sea, California, and South Africa, focusing on high-value crops like olives, grapes, citrus fruits, and winter vegetables that thrive in mild, wet winters and hot, dry summers.
