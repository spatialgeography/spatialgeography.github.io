## Thompson's Model (1929) — 3 Categories

| Category | Characteristics |
|:---|:---|
| 1st | Fertility and mortality rates are very high |
| 2nd | Fertility and mortality rates begin to decline |
| 3rd | Rapidly declining birth rates — slowing down population growth |

---

## Notestein's Model (1945) — 3 Stages

| Stage | Countries | Description |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **1st Stage** | Most of Asia, Africa, Latin America | Very high birth rate and death rate |
| **2nd Stage** | Soviet Union, Japan, some Latin American countries | 'Transitional Growth' |
| **Final Stage** | USA, most of Europe, Australia, New Zealand | 'Incipient Decline' — birth rate falling |

---

## C.P. Blacker's Model (1947) — 5 Stages

| Stage | Name | Birth Rate | Death Rate |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| 1 | **High Stationary** | High | High |
| 2 | **Early Expanding** | High | Rapidly declining |
| 3 | **Late Expanding** | Declining | Rapidly declining |
| 4 | **Low Stationary** | Low | Low |
| 5 | **Declining** | Both at low level | Both at low level |

---

## W.S. Thompson's 3 Hypotheses
1. Decline in **mortality comes before** decline in fertility
2. Fertility **eventually declines** to match mortality
3. Socio-economic transformation of a society takes place **simultaneously** with its demographic transformation

# Part 4: Migration Theories

## 1. Ravenstein's Laws of Migration (1885)

> E.G. Ravenstein — *'Laws of Migration'* (1885)

- Migrants move mainly over **short distances**; those going longer distances go for industry and commerce
- Most migration is from **agricultural to industrial** areas
- Large towns grow more by **migration** than by natural increase
- Migration increases with development of **industry, commerce and transport**
- Each migration stream produces a **counter-stream**
- **Females** are more migratory than males over shorter distances; males dominate in **international migration**
- **Major cause of migration is economic**
- Most migration is from **rural to urban** areas
- Most migration proceeds **step by stage**

---

## 2. Push–Pull Theory — E.S. Lee (1966)

| Factor | Description | Examples |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Push Factors** | Reasons to move OUT of origin | Poverty, political instability, natural disasters |
| **Pull Factors** | Reasons to move INTO destination | Economic opportunity, jobs, better education |
| **Intervening Obstacles** | Barriers between origin and destination | Distance, cost, immigration laws, natural inertia |

### Lee's Hypotheses
- Volume of migration within a given territory varies with the diversity of that territory
- Volume of migration is related to the **difficulty of surmounting** the intervening obstacles

---

## 3. Gravity Model — W.J. Reilly (1929)

> *"Migration is directly proportional to the product of their population size and inversely proportional to the square of the distance separating them"*

> **Formula:** \[ M_{ij} = k \cdot rac{P_i \cdot P_j}{d_{ij}^2} \]

- Initially proposed by **Social Physics**
- W.J. Reilly (1929) — first proposed
- Reilly's **'Law of Retail Gravitation'**

## 4. Intervening Opportunities — Stouffer (1940)

- **S.A. Stouffer (1940)** — *Intervening Opportunity Model*
- **No relation** between mobility and distance
- Migration depends on number of intervening opportunities
- Formula: \[ y = rac{V_x}{x} k \]

## 5. Zelinsky's Migration Transition Model

> Related with Demographic Transition Theory

| Stage | Society | Type of Migration |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Stage 1** | Pre-urbanisation / Pre-modern | No significant migration |
| **Stage 2** | Early Traditional Society | Rural → Urban migration |
| **Stage 3** | Late Transitional Society | Urban → Urban migration |
| **Stage 4** | Advanced Society | Within-city migration; Urban agglomeration / Counter-urbanisation |
| **Stage 5** | Future Super-Advanced Society | All migration between or within society |

# Part 5: Population Projection

> **Population Projection** — calculation of future birth rate, death rate and migration

## Types of Projection
1. Total Projection and Regional Projection
2. Forward Projections and Backward Projections
3. High, Medium and Low Projection

## History
- **1835 — Quetelet** propounded the method:
  *"The resistance or the sum of the obstacles opposed to the ultimate growth of population increases"*

## Methods of Projection

### 1. Arithmetic Method
> \[ P_p = P_t + rac{n(P_1 - P_2)}{N} \]
> Pp = Projected Population; Pt = base population; P1 = present; P2 = previous census; N = number of years between censuses

### 2. Geometric Method
> \[ P_p = P_1(1 + r)^n \]
> Pp = Projected Population; P1 = population in census year; r = annual rate of increase; n = number of years

### 3. Growth Component Method
- More practical than mathematical method
- Considers fertility, mortality and migration separately

### 4. Economic Method
- Studies how and to what extent birth, death and migration rates are affected by economic factors

## Importance of Population Projection
1. For Economic Development
2. For Social Development
3. For business class / private sector
4. Demographic Theory and planning

# Part 8: Population Resource Regions

## Concept
- Studied in relation to **resource availability** and **carrying capacity**
- Population–resource relationship first studied systematically by **Malthus (1798)**

## Malthusian Theory (1798)
- *"Population grows geometrically (1, 2, 4, 8...) but food supply grows arithmetically (1, 2, 3, 4...)"*
- Population tends to outstrip food supply → **poverty, famine, disease** as positive checks
- **Preventive checks:** Celibacy, late marriage, moral restraint
- **Positive checks:** War, famine, disease (increase death rate)

## Optimum Population Theory
- There exists an **optimum level** of population for any given area
- Below optimum → under-population; above optimum → over-population
- Best level where **per capita income is maximised**

# Part 10: Settlement Geography

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|:---|:---|
| **Settlement** | Any form of human habitation — from single dwelling to largest city |
| **Rural Settlement** | Settlements dependent on primary activities; low density |
| **Urban Settlement** | Settlements dependent on secondary/tertiary activities; high density |

## Types of Rural Settlements

| Type | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Clustered / Nucleated** | Houses close together; common in India |
| **Dispersed / Scattered** | Houses far apart; common in W. Europe, USA |
| **Linear** | Along roads, rivers or coastlines |
| **Hamleted** | Fragmented into sub-hamlets |

## Urban Settlement Hierarchy
City → Town → Borough → Municipality → Village
