## Fundamentals of Agricultural Geography


Agricultural geography focuses on the spatial distribution of agricultural activities and the multitude of factors that influence farming choices across different regions. It acts as the bridge between human societies and their physical environments through the lens of food production.

### Key Principles
* **Factors Affecting Agriculture**: Agricultural patterns are shaped by a complex interplay of physical factors (climate, soil fertility, topography, and water availability) and human factors (labor availability, capital investment, technological access, and government policies).
* **Agricultural Regions (Whittlesey)**: Derwent Whittlesey's classic 1936 classification identified 13 distinct agricultural regions of the world based on crop types, livestock associations, labor intensity, and the ultimate destination of the produce.
* **Intensive vs. Extensive Agriculture**: Intensive farming involves high inputs of capital and labor relative to land area (e.g., wet rice cultivation in Asia), aiming for maximum yield per hectare. Extensive farming uses vast tracts of land with relatively low inputs per area (e.g., commercial wheat farming in the American Midwest), focusing on yield per capita.
* **Subsistence vs. Commercial Agriculture**: Subsistence agriculture is designed primarily to provide food for direct consumption by the farmer and their family, typical in developing nations. Commercial agriculture focuses on cash-crop production for sale off the farm, dominating the developed world.
:::
