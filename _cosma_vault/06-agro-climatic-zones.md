## Agro-Climatic Regions of India

Agro-climatic regional planning was initiated in India by the Planning Commission to achieve a balanced regional development of agriculture.

### Classification by Planning Commission (1989)
The Planning Commission of India divided the country into **15 major agro-climatic regions** based on soil type, rainfall, temperature, and water resources.

1.  **Western Himalayan Region**: High altitude, temperate climate.
2.  **Eastern Himalayan Region**: High rainfall, undulating terrain.
3.  **Lower Gangetic Plain**: High humidity, paddy cultivation.
4.  **Middle Gangetic Plain**: Fertile alluvial soils.
5.  **Upper Gangetic Plain**: Significant irrigation (canals/tubewells).
6.  **Trans-Gangetic Plain**: Semiarid, highly mechanized (Punjab/Haryana).
7.  **Eastern Plateau and Hills**: Red soils, rice/millets.
8.  **Central Plateau and Hills**: Mixed soils, moisture-deficient.
9.  **Western Plateau and Hills**: Black cotton soils (Regur).
10. **Southern Plateau and Hills**: Dry farming, millets.
11. **East Coast Plains and Hills**: Deltaic soils, marine resources.
12. **West Coast Plains and Ghats**: High rainfall, plantation crops.
13. **Gujarat Plains and Hills**: Oilseeds, cotton.
14. **Western Dry Region**: Arid (Thar Desert), livestock.
15. **Islands Region**: Tropical, marine resources (Andaman & Nicobar, Lakshadweep).

### Objectives
*   To attempt a broad demand-supply balance of major commodities at the national level.
*   To maximize the net income of producers.
*   To generate additional employment, particularly for landless laborers.
*   To provide a framework for scientific and sustainable use of natural resources.
