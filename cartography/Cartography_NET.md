## Page 2

**Thematic Map**  
Mainly 4 types of map are listed here. [file:1]

**Topographic Maps**  
- Include contour lines to show the shape and elevation of an area.  
- Lines close together show steep terrain, while lines far apart show flat terrain.  
- Geologists use topographic maps to record rock types.  
- Engineers use them when planning roads, buildings, and other structures. [file:1]

**Thematic Maps**  
- Can represent climate, precipitation, vegetation, elevation, population, life expectancy, and more.  
- Usually used when looking at a single piece of information.  
- Easy to understand. [file:1]

**Types**  
- Choropleth map - regions are colored, shaded, or patterned in relation to value.  
- Heat map - color represents intensity, but unlike choropleth maps it does not use geographical boundaries.  
- Proportional symbol map - symbols represent data at points and are scaled by value, such as circles or pies.  
- Dot density map - used to represent a feature or attribute in data.  
- Animated time series map - used when data has a temporal context. [file:1]

**Other examples**  
- National Resource Map  
- Vegetation Cover Map  
- Groundwater Map  
- Population Density Map  
- Average Wind Speed Map  
- HDI Map [file:1]

## Page 3

**Physical Map**  
- Shows natural features of the earth such as mountains, plateaus, plains, rivers, and oceans.  
- Also called relief maps.  
- Physical maps do not contain man-made features.  
- Green is usually used at lower elevations, and orange or brown at higher elevations.  
- Example: a view as if looking down on Earth from space. [file:1]

**Political Map**  
- Shows cities, towns, villages, countries, and states with their boundaries.  
- Does not show physical features.  
- Capital cities are usually marked with a star within a circle.  
- May be black and white or use color to distinguish countries. [file:1]

## Page 4

**Scales**  
- Verbal Scale  
- Graphic Scale  
- Representative Fraction [file:1]

**Verbal Scale**  
Expresses in words the relationship between map distance and ground distance, for example one inch represents 16 miles. [file:1]

**Graphic Scale / Bar Scale**  
Shows directly on the map the corresponding ground distance. [file:1]

**Representative Fraction (RF)**  
- The most abstract and versatile scale form.  
- Shows the relationship between one unit on the map and the same unit on the ground.  
- Example: 1:25000. [file:1]

**Scale size and RF**  
- Large scale: 1:25000 or larger.  
- Medium scale: 1:1000,000 to 1:25000.  
- Small scale: 1:1000,000 or smaller. [file:1]

**Flow Map**  
Used to measure the degree of concentration of traffic, goods, and passengers along different lines of communication. [file:1]

**Choropleth Map**  
- Suitable for showing economic and geographical phenomena.  
- Uses color or shading.  
- Color and shading should be chosen carefully for effective visual effect. [file:1]

**Chorochromatic Map**  
- Shows non-quantitative areal distribution by shading and tinting.  
- Also known as the colored or tint method.  
- Also called the layering method.  
- Non-quantitative areal distributions may also be shown using symbols such as dots, circles, triangles, or index letters like T for Tea and R for Rice. [file:1]

**Choroschematic Map**  
- Shows non-quantitative areal distribution by symbols, index letters, and related devices.  
- Prepared when regional variations are to be conveyed effectively. [file:1]

## Page 7

**Chorochromatic vs Choropleth**  
- Both represent fields and can be constructed from the same data.  
- The main difference lies in the nature of the data. [file:1]

- In a choropleth map, the variable is summarized over predetermined districts.  
- In a chorochromatic map, regions are drawn based on detailed locational data. [file:1]

**Chorochromatic Maps**  
- From Greek terms related to area and color.  
- Also known as area class maps or qualitative area maps.  
- Map nominal data using symbols, colors, black and white, or patterns.  
- Colors are mapped according to data boundaries.  
- Only nominal data should be graphed, with no indication of hierarchy or order.  
- Common examples include soil maps and biome maps. [file:1]

## Page 8

**Star Diagram**  
- Used for organizing characteristics of a single topic.  
- A central space displays the topic, while each point of the star lists a fact, attribute, or trait.  
- Examples include radar charts and wind rose diagrams. [file:1]

**Scatter Diagram**  
- Used to investigate the relationship between two variables for paired data.  
- Scatter pattern shows the relationship.  
- Common forms include no correlation, weak positive correlation, strong positive correlation, and strong negative correlation. [file:1]

**Spearman's Rank Correlation Coefficient**  
\(R = 1 - 6\Sigma d^2 / n(n^2 - 1)\) [file:1]

**Interpretation of R**  
- -1 = perfect negative  
- +1 = perfect positive  
- 0.00 to 0.19 = very weak correlation  
- 0.20 to 0.39 = weak correlation  
- 0.40 to 0.69 = moderate correlation  
- 0.70 to 0.89 = strong correlation  
- 0.90 to 1.00 = very strong correlation [file:1]

**Hythergraph**  
- Associated with Griffith Taylor.  
- Shows the relationship between temperature and rainfall.  
- Important for comparing the climatic character of different regions. [file:1]

**Triangulation Graph**  
- Data is represented in a three-sided triangular graph.  
- Each axis is divided into 100 percent.  
- This method helps determine the exact location. [file:1]

**Toposheet**  
Topographic maps are detailed and accurate graphic representations of features on the earth's surface. [file:1]

**Major elements shown**  
- Cultural: roads, buildings, urban development, railways, states, international borders.  
- Hydrography: lakes, rivers, streams, swamps, coastal flats.  
- Relief: mountains, valleys, contours, cliffs, depressions.  
- Vegetation: wooded and cleared areas, vineyards, orchards. [file:1]

**Contour**  
- Joins points of equal elevation.  
- The vertical distance between two consecutive contours is called the contour interval. [file:1]

**Survey of India (SOI)**  
- Prints maps at 1:250,000, 1:50,000, and 1:2500.  
- Based on Everest datum and Polyconic Projection.  
- OSM means Open Series Map, publicly available, based on WGS-84 datum and UTM projection. [file:1]

**OSM and DSM**  
- OSM = Open Series Map, WGS 84, UTM.  
- DSM = Defence Series Map, WGS 84, LLC. [file:1]

**DSM**  
- Supports national security.  
- Uses WGS 84 datum and LLC projection. [file:1]

**General Wall Maps and State Maps**  
- General wall maps: 1:12M scale.  
- State maps: 1:1M scale. [file:1]

**Dot Maps**  
- A dot is used to represent features or phenomena.  
- Dot maps rely on visual scatter to show spatial pattern.  
- One-to-one: each dot represents a single recorded phenomenon.  
- One-to-many: each dot may represent more than one phenomenon. [file:1]

**Types of contour-related lines**  
- A contour line is a curve along which a function has a constant value.  
- Contour lines often have names beginning with iso, meaning equal. [file:1]

**Examples**  
- Equidistant points: points at the same distance from a given point.  
- Isopleths: contours for variables that cannot be measured at a point and must be calculated over an area, such as population density.  
- Isobar: line of equal pressure.  
- Isallobar: line of equal pressure change during a specific time interval. [file:1]
