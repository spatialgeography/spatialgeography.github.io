Population Geography

Spatial distribution, density, growth, composition, and migration of human populations.

Author

Geography Team

Welcome to the Population Geography module of Geography OpenCourseWare. This course studies the spatial dimensions of human population — including distribution, density, growth trends, demographic transitions, migration, and their implications for development.

UGC NET Syllabus

  • Sources of population data (census, sample surveys and vital statistics, data reliability and errors).
  • World Population Distribution (measures, patterns and determinants), World Population Growth (prehistoric to modern period).
  • Demographic Transition, Theories of Population Growth (Malthus, Sadler, and Ricardo).
  • Fertility and Mortality Analysis (indices, determinants and world patterns).
  • Migration (types, causes and consequences and models).
  • Population Composition and Characteristics (age, sex, rural-urban, occupational structure and educational levels).
  • Population Policies in Developed and Developing Countries.

Where people live and why.

NoteSpatial Patterns

Population Growth

Trends and dynamics of population increase.

Get the Presentation ↗   |   Watch the Video ↗

TipGrowth Patterns
  • World Population Growth: From 1 billion (1800) to 8 billion (2022) — the demographic explosion.
  • Components of Growth: Birth rate (CBR), death rate (CDR), and natural increase (RNI).
  • Fertility Measures: Total Fertility Rate (TFR), General Fertility Rate, and replacement-level fertility.
  • Mortality Measures: Infant Mortality Rate (IMR), Maternal Mortality Ratio, and life expectancy.
  • Doubling Time: Time required for a population to double at current growth rates.
  • India’s Population Growth: Census trends — from 361 million (1951) to 1.4 billion (2021).

Demographic Transition

The model linking economic development to population change.

Get the Presentation ↗   |   Watch the Video ↗

ImportantTransition Stages
  • Stage 1 (Pre-Transition): High birth rates, high death rates — slow growth (pre-industrial societies).
  • Stage 2 (Early Transition): High births, declining deaths — rapid growth (improving healthcare/sanitation).
  • Stage 3 (Late Transition): Declining births, low deaths — decelerating growth (industrialization/urbanization).
  • Stage 4 (Post-Transition): Low births, low deaths — near-zero growth (developed nations).
  • Stage 5 (Decline): Sub-replacement fertility — negative natural increase (Japan, Germany, Italy).
  • India’s Position: States at different transition stages — Kerala (Stage 4) vs. Bihar (Stage 2-3).

Population Composition

The structure and characteristics of populations.

Get the Presentation ↗   |   Watch the Video ↗

NoteDemographic Structure
  • Age-Sex Structure: Population pyramids — expansive, constrictive, and stationary types.
  • Dependency Ratio: Youth and old-age dependency — and the demographic dividend.
  • Sex Ratio: Gender imbalance — global patterns and India’s declining child sex ratio.
  • Literacy & Education: Geographic variation in literacy rates and their development implications.
  • Occupational Structure: Primary-, secondary-, and tertiary-sector workforce distribution.
  • Ethnic & Linguistic Composition: Diversity, plurality, and the geography of languages and religions.

Migration

The movement of people across space.

Get the Presentation ↗   |   Watch the Video ↗

TipMigration Dynamics
  • Types of Migration: Internal (rural-urban, intra-state) and international (emigration, immigration).
  • Push & Pull Factors: Economic opportunity, safety, education, environment, and family.
  • Ravenstein’s Laws of Migration: Classic generalizations about migration patterns.
  • Lee’s Theory of Migration: Intervening obstacles, personal factors, and origin-destination dynamics.
  • Refugees & Forced Migration: Conflict, persecution, and environmental displacement.
  • Indian Migration Patterns: Rural-urban migration, seasonal labor migration, and the Gulf diaspora.

Population Policies

Government interventions in population dynamics.

Get the Presentation ↗   |   Watch the Video ↗

ImportantPolicy Approaches
  • Pro-Natalist Policies: France, Singapore — incentives for higher fertility.
  • Anti-Natalist Policies: China’s One-Child Policy, India’s Family Planning Programme.
  • India’s National Population Policy (2000): Goals, strategies, and outcomes.
  • Population & Development: Cairo ICPD (1994) — reproductive health and women’s empowerment.
  • Malthusian vs. Anti-Malthusian Debate: Population growth vs. resource availability — Malthus, Boserup, Marx, and Club of Rome.
