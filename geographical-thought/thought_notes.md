# Part 1: Ancient \& Medieval Period

## Greek Scholars — "Golden Period"

- Sexagesimal system = 360 days/year, 12 months of 30 days each
- Algebra borrowed from Mesopotamians \& Sumerians
- Invented the first phonetic alphabets
- Established port 'Carthage' at the shores of the Mediterranean Sea
- Sumerians divided the circle into 360 degrees


### Homer (900–701 BCE)

- Works: *Odyssey* and *Iliad*
- Identified winds from different directions:
    - Boreas (North), Eurus (East), Notus (South), Zephyrus (West)


### Thales of Miletus (600–500 BCE)

- One of the legendary Seven Wise Men (*Sophoi*)
- Believed the Earth was a flat disk floating on a vast sea/water
- Established mathematical traditions
- First to state measurement of earth and location of things on the face of the earth
- **Six Basic Theorems of Geometry:**

1. The circle is divided into equal parts by its diameter
2. Angles in a triangle opposite two sides of equal length are equal
3. Opposite angles formed by intersecting straight lines are equal
4. A triangle inscribed inside a semicircle is a right angle
5. The sides of similar triangles are proportional
6. A triangle is determined if its base and the two angles at the base are given
- A critic of traditional unscientific explanations


### Anaximander (610–546 BCE) — Cosmologist

- *Gnomon*: a Babylonian instrument at Sparta — a pole set vertically above a flat surface to measure varying positions of sun and celestial bodies
- Produced a world map to scale; connected to the Milesian school


### Hecataeus (600–480 BCE) — Miletus

- *Ges-periodos*: or "Description of the Earth" (Mediterranean Sea)
    - Dealing with geographical information about Europe
    - Dealing with Libya
- First to give two approaches in Geography:

1. **Nomothetic Approach**
2. **Idiographic Approach**
- Two parts of the map — Northern Part (Europe) \& Southern Part (Asia)


### Herodotus (484–420 BCE)

- Father of History; Father of Ethnography
- "History must be treated geographically and all geography must be treated historically"
- First to state that wind moves from cold to hot places
- Calculated age of earth at 440 million years from sediment (1 foot = 880 years; 158 km = 440 million years)


### Plato (428–348 BCE)

- Master of **Deductive Reasoning** (general to particular)
- Believed the rounded earth is located in the center of the universe with celestial bodies revolving around it
- Works: *Theaetetus*, *Letters*, *Critias*, *Menexenus*
- Established that the Earth is spherical in shape


### Aristotle (384–322 BCE)

- Pioneer of **Inductive Reasoning**
- First teleologist
- **Torrid Zone** — near equator, uninhabitable region
- **Frigid Zone** — permanently frozen, also uninhabitable
- People of Earth lived between these two zones


### Eratosthenes (276–194 BCE)

- **Father of Geography**
- Coined the word 'Geography' — *ge* (Earth) + *graphy* (graphein, to describe)
- Defined geography as the "Study of Earth as the home of man"
- Measured Earth's circumference
- Book: *Geographica*
- Produced a world map with correct distances
- Five Climatic Zones: One Torrid, Two Temperate, Two Frigid
- **Father of Geodesy** — measured different latitudes and longitudes


### Hippocrates (460–375 BCE)

- *On Airs, Waters, Places* — studied the man-nature relationship


### Hipparchus (190–127 BCE)

- First to divide the circle into 360°
- Established the Equator as a great circle
- Stated Earth makes a complete revolution in 24 hours
- Developed Map Projection — Stereographic and Orthographic projection
- *Astrolabe* — an instrument to measure latitude at sea
- *Climata* — divided the world on the basis of latitudes
- Known as Founder of Trigonometry; famous for the discovery of equinoxes
- Calculated the length of the year to within six and a half minutes
- Compiled the first known star catalog in 129 BC — 850 stars listed
- Applied rigorous mathematical principles to the determination of places on the earth surface
- **Stereographic projection** — by laying a flat plane tangent to earth and extending lat/long lines from a point opposite the point of tangency
- **Orthographic projection** — by projecting lines from a point in infinity


### Posidonius (135–51 BCE)

- Calculated Earth's circumference (smaller than Eratosthenes)
- Tried to calculate diameter of the Earth by influence of the moon
- **Father of Oceanography**

## Roman Scholars

### Strabo (64 BC – 21 AD)

- *Geographia* — 17 volumes (8: Europe, 6: Asia, 1: Africa)
- First volume devoted to development of geography
- *The Ekumene*: Zones of habitable world
- Laid the foundation of Chronological paradigm in Geography


### Ptolemy (100–170 AD)

- "Geography was a science of the art of map-making"
- *The Almagest* — study of movement of celestial bodies
- Improved map-making science by adopting projections
- Accepted the view of spherical shape of Earth
- *Guide to Geography* — 8 volumes discussing map projections
- Volumes 2–7: Latitude and Longitude
- Accepted the view of uninhabitable zones
- Comprehensive view of Earth

## Alexander Von Humboldt (1769–1859)

- German; Pioneer of Physical Geography
- Contributed in geology, botany, zoology, physics, chemistry, anatomy, physiology, history, climatology, geomorphology, and all other branches of geography
- Studied at Frankfurt, University of Göttingen
- **1798** → With French botanist: made daily observations of temperature and altitudes in Madrid
- **1800** → Explored the Orinoco river
- **1801** → Arrived at Colombia (Cartagena)
- Climbed Mountain Chimborazo Peak (world record time)
- First to observe and record the cold water current of Peru
- **1806** → Observed the Vesuvius Volcano
- **1829** → Invited by Russian Czar
- Concept of **'Continentality'** — Temperature on the same latitudes varies moving inward from the coast
- *Permafrost* — term coined to describe frozen Siberian soil
- Word **'Climatology'** appeared through his work


### Key Works — *Kosmos* (1845), 4 Volumes

| Volume | Content |
| :-- | :-- |
| 1st | General presentation of the whole picture of the universe |
| 2nd | Portrayals of nature through the ages; history of man's effort to describe earth science |
| 3rd | Laws of celestial space (Astronomy) |

### Key Concepts

- **Cosmography** — divided into:
    - *Uranography* — descriptive astronomy (celestial bodies)
    - *Geography* — physical geography (terrestrial part)
- **Zusammenhang** — concept of "hanging together"
- **Causal Connection** — unity of nature; inherent causality
- Believed in **'Unified Universal Science'** encompassing all physical, biological and social sciences
- "Man everywhere becomes most essentially associated with terrestrial life"
- All races of man had a common origin — no race is superior or inferior to others
- Humboldt was the **last master of universal science**


### Three Different Things to Mankind (Humboldt)

- A lofty moral idealism common to religion
- A geological dream regarding the origin of earth
- A legend concerning the origins of religion

***

## Carl Ritter (1779–1859)

- Believed in empirical research; a teleologist (strong believer in God)
- **1807** → Met Humboldt for the first time
- **1814** → University of Göttingen; **1818** → University of Frankfurt; **1820** → Professor of Geography
- Developed concept of **"Unity in Diversity"**
- ***Erdkunde*** — "earth science" (science of earth in relation to nature and history)
    - 1st attribute: Topographical — natural divisions of earth science
    - 2nd attribute: Distribution and movement of water, atmosphere, and base of human life
    - 1817–1859: Completed 19 volumes of *Erdkunde*
- **Founder of Comprehensive Model of Regional Geography**
- "The central principle of geography is the relation of all phenomena and forms of nature to the human race"
- First great opponent of "armchair geography"
- **Deductive method** — deduces new conclusions from fundamental assumptions
- Defined continents with different races and colors:
    - Africa — Black people
    - Europe — White people
    - Asia — Yellow people
    - America — Red people
- "As body is made of soul, so is the physical globe made for mankind"
- Greatest contributor in the field of regional geography

***

## Charles Darwin (1809–1882)

- *Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection* (1859)
- *Descent of Man and Selection in Relation to Sex* (1871)
- *Variation in Animals and Plants under Domestication* (1868)
- Influenced by Charles Lyell's *Principle of Geology*
- Influenced by Malthus (1798) — theory of evolution by natural selection
- Explored taxonomy of branches, formation of atolls and barrier reefs, role of earthworms in soil fertility
- Darwin rejected the teleological concept of Ritter
- Influenced **Oscar Peschel** → *New Problems of Comparative Geography as a Search for Morphology of Earth Surface* (1870)


### Darwin's Influence on Geography

- 19th century = period of "geologification"
- W.M. Davis concept of **'Cycle of Erosion'** — landforms evolve like organic life
- ***Landschaft*** — geography as landscape science
- Ratzel (follower of Darwin) → *Anthropogeographie* — "Similar location leads to similar mode of life"
- E.C. Semple → *Influence of Geographic Environment* — "man is the product of earth surface"
- **'Lebensraum'** (Living Space) concept influenced by Darwin — coined by Ratzel in *Political Geography*
- **'Cultural Landscape'** — Carl O. Sauer → article *The Morphology of Landscape*

# Part 3: National Schools of Geographical Thought

## German School

### Oscar Peschel (1826–1875)

- German Geographer; Professor at University of Leipzig
- *Das Ausland* — a periodical publishing articles about geography of foreign countries
- Laid the foundation of **Modern Physical Geography**
- Primarily concerned with study of Landform


### Ferdinand Von Richthofen (1833–1905)

- Trained as Geologist; field studies in China, California
- Taught Geography at Bonn, Leipzig, and Berlin
- **Founder of modern geomorphology**
- "The purpose of geography is to focus attention on the diverse phenomena that occur in interrelation on the face of earth"
- First German scholar to differentiate between General and Regional Geography:
    - **Regional Geography** — descriptive, highlighting salient features of a region
    - **General Geography** — deals with spatial distribution and explanation of individual phenomena in the world
- Different methods for studying areas of different size:
    - *Endteile* — major divisions of the world
    - *Lander* — major regions
    - *Landschofen* — Landscape or small region
    - *Ortlichkeiten* — Localities


### Friedrich Ratzel (1844–1904)

- Trained in Zoology and Geology at Munich
- **Universally recognized as the founder of Human Geography**
- Visited Italy (1872), USA and Mexico (1874–75), Europe
- Taught at University of Munich and Leipzig
- Known for applying Darwin's biological concepts to Human Geography
- *Anthropogeography* — coined after fieldwork among tribes, races, and nations
    - **1st Volume** — study of man-earth relationship; deductive approach
    - **2nd Volume** — influence of man on environment; physical environment controls human activities
- *Political Geography* (1897) — state as an organism; philosophy of **Lebensraum** (Living Space)
- **Lebensraum** — "when a state expands its frontiers at the expense of a weak state, the superior nation rules the inferior peoples, fulfilling a natural organic necessity"
- **Social Darwinism** — applied Darwin's biological concepts in human societies
- **Basic Philosophy** → "Survival of the fittest" in a physical environment
- Ratzel's view of progress of human society: ① Hunting \& fishing → ② Hoe culture → ③ Crops cultivation → ④ Mixed farming → ⑤ Unmixed herding → ⑥ Horticulture
- Pupil: **Ellen Churchill Semple**


### Alfred Hettner (1859–1941)

- Pupil of Richthofen and Ratzel; taught at Leipzig
- A physical and regional geographer
- *Europe* (1907) — supported Strabo's view of geography as a chorological science
- **Chorological Concept of Geography** — geographer must study both immaterial (race, ethnicity, religion, language, customs) and material phenomena
- "Geography is a field in which things have to be described in their areal context, much as history is in their time context"
- Claimed geography is **ideographic (regional)** rather than nomothetic (systematic)
- Advocated concept: **"Unity of geography is in method"**
- Procedure of study: from 'man to nature' rather than 'nature to man'
- Geography must deal in both unique (regional) and universal (general) things; but study of region is the main field


#### Landschafts Kunde (Landscape Science)

- Gained popularity in Germany at the beginning of the 20th century
- Existing landscape of a meso or micro region = result of exogenetic, historical and cultural forces


### Albrecht Penck (1858–1945)

- Formulated the concept of **'Geomorphology'**
- Professor of Physical Geography at Vienna (1885), Professor at Berlin (1906–26)
- Associated with Eduard Suess — prepared maps of major geological regions of the world
- Founded principles of landforms evolution
- First to postulate that 'evaporation increases with higher temperature'
- Considered man an important agent who carves out the face of the earth
- *Gestalt* — introduced the term borrowed from Psychology in Geography (unified form of larger areas)
- "Geography is a bridge between natural and social science"


### Gerhard Schott

- Studied motion, temperature, salinity, colour, ocean deposits, configuration, ocean climate, geological structure of ocean basins, ocean organisms, ocean trade routes and air routes over ocean

## French School

### Paul Vidal de la Blache (1845–1918)

- **Founder of Human Geography** (France)
- Taught at University of Nancy (1872–77), École (1891), University of Sorbonne (Paris) for 26 years
- *Annales de Géographie* — his periodical
- **1894** — *Atlas Générale Vidal Lablache* (1st vol.)
- *Pays* — homogenous regions in France
- Influenced by Ratzel's writings
- **Possibilism** — from the 2nd Volume of *Anthropogeographie*
- "Nature is never more than an advisor"
- Endorsed by historian Lucien Febvre: "There are no necessities but everywhere possibilities"
- Conceived earth surface as the terrestrial organism
- Coined concept **'Genre de Vie'** (Life Style/Way of Life) — includes complex of institutions, traditions, attitudes, purposes and technical skill of people
- Book: *Tableau de la France* — harmonious blending of physical and human features
- Criticized taking river basin as the unit of study; 'pays' (regions) are basic objectives of study
- *Human Geography* (1921) — completed by his son-in-law Emmanuel de Martonne
- **'Regional geography is the core of geography'** — Blache
- "Man is the most adaptable and mobile organism on the face of the Earth"
- Father of Human Geography who advocated Possibilism


#### Principle of Terrestrial Unity

- The dominant idea in all geographical process is terrestrial unity
- Vegetation not only accentuates landforms but gives landscape character — steppe, savanna, silva, taiga are collective terms


### Jean Brunhes (1869–1930)

- Disciple of Vidal-de-la-Blache
- *Géographie Humaine: Essai de classification Positive* (1910)
- Limited human geography to:

1. Unproductive occupation of soil
2. Things connected with the conquest of plant and animal worlds
3. The destructive economy — 'robber economy' or violent attack on nature
- Two principles of geographical study:
    - **Principle of Activity** — physical and cultural phenomena are in perpetual change; must be studied in temporal change
    - **Principle of Interaction** — geographical phenomena (physical and social) are closely interrelated


### Elisée Reclus (1830–1905)

- A student of Ritter (most successful)
- First and foremost an uncompromising idealist
- Supported the 1848 revolution
- Travelled North, South and Central America, Colombia
- Leading promoter of **anarchism** (friend of Mikhail Bakunin)
- Professor at *New University of Brussels*
- Works:
    - *La Terre* — systematic physical geography
    - *Nouvelle Géographie Universelle* (1875–94) 19 Vol — best known works
    - *L'Homme et la Terre* (1905–08) — social geography; historical account of humanity's life on earth
- Close friend of Patrick Geddes
- Called "the Ritter of France"
- "Man is not the product of his environment but an important part of it"


### Emmanuel de Martonne (1873–1955)

- Dominated Vidalian approach; pupil and son-in-law of Blache
- Specialized in physical geography — particularly central Europe
- Studied problems of glacial erosion of the Alps
- **1904** — W.M. Davis was impressed by his work at the 8th *International Geographical Congress*
- Works: *Traité de Géographie Physique*, *La France Physique*


### Albert Demangeon (1872–1940)

- Pupil of Vidal-de-la-Blache
- Monograph: *La Picardie et les Religions Voisines*
- Appointed at University of Lille
- Made valuable contributions to spatial variations of farmsteads
- Contributed to editing *Annales*

## British School

### Halford J. Mackinder (1861–1947)

- **Founder of British School of Geography**
- First person to climb Mt. Kenya at second time
- Vocal supporter of geographical education at Oxford University
- "The subject had to bridge natural science and humanities, with its core being the interaction of man in society and environment varies locally"
- **1887** — Appointed Professor of Geography at Oxford University
- Geography as a discipline tracing the interaction of man with his physical environment


#### Heartland Theory (1904)

- Concept of *the geographical pivot of history*
- **'World Island'** — consisting of countries of Eurasia \& Africa
- > "Who rules East Europe, commands the Heartland; who rules the Heartland commands the World Island; who rules the World Island commands the World."
- Coastal lands proved vulnerable to attack from the heartland; the heartland remained invulnerable to sea power
- Supporter of **Environmental Determinism**


#### Key Works

- **1902** — *Britain and British Seas* — classic in modern British literature; regional interpretation of Britain
- **1919** — *Democratic Ideals and Reality* — discusses world power politics


### Other British Geographers

- **H. Robert Mill** — *Realm of Nature* — discusses races of man
- **Patrick Geddes** — *Place, Work and Folk* — basic concepts for study of cities and regions; founder of *La Play Society*
- **Andrew J. Herbertson** — scheme of division of world into natural regions based on surface features, climate and vegetation
- **A.G. Ogilive** — edited *Great Britain: Essay in Regional Geography* (1928)


### Economic Geography

- **Buchanan** → *Pastoral Industries of New Zealand* — influence of physical environment on economic conditions
- **W. Smith** → *Economic Geography of Britain* (1949) — systematic investigation of regional variation of economies
- **Chisholm** → *Handbook of Commercial Geography*
- **Robert Mill** → *General Geography*; appointed director of British Rainfall Organisation (1901)
- **1930** → L.D. Stamp started preparing land use map of Britain


### Contemporary Trends (British)

- Major changes in the form of **'Quantitative Revolution'**
- **Chorley \& Haggett** — explained space relation and location analysis
    - Books: *Models in Human Geography*, *Locational Analysis in Human Geography*, *Frontiers in Geographical Teaching*
- **RGS** = Royal Geographical Society — sponsors scientific expeditions; publishes *Geographical Journal*

## American School

### William Morris Davis (1850–1934)

- Born in Philadelphia; Harvard (1869)
- **1870–73** → Worked at Argentina Meteorological Observatory
- **1899** — Professor of Geography
- **Founder of AAG (American Association of Geographers) 1904**
- **1877** — Observed Montana; gave concept of **'Cycle of Erosion'** (geomorphic cycle / topographical cycle)
    - Presented theory at **IGC (International Geographical Congress) 1899**
- Students of Davis: Mark Jefferson, Isaiah Bowman, Ellsworth Huntington, Ellen Churchill Semple, Albert Brigham
- *Ontography* — ecological study of human groups


### Mark Jefferson (1863–1949)

- Studied with W.M. Davis; prolific writer
- **Founder of real man-oriented geography**
- *The Civilizing Rails* — examined size, extent and functions of cities and railroad access areas
- Coined ideas of *Central Places*, **Laws of Primate City**, and *Civilizing Rails*
- Geographical concern: "Where men are, what they are like, and why they are there"


### Ellen Churchill Semple (1863–1932)

- Foremost female geographer of her time; leading exponent of **Environmental Determinism**
- Pupil and follower of Ratzel
- Works:
    - *Influences of Geographic Environment* (1911) — "Man is a product of the surface of the Earth"
    - *American History and its Conditions* (1903)
- Visited Siberia, Mongolia, Japan, China, Philippines, Java, India, European countries
- "Location means climate and plant life at one end of the scale, civilization and political status at the other"
- "Geography lies at the basis of history"


### Albert Perry Brigham (1855–1932)

- Interested in geology and geomorphology
- Joined Harvard with geologist Shaler
- Against vague and unproved assertions of climatic influence on social character
- Helped run AAG


### Carl O. Sauer (1889–1975)

- University of California (most of his life)
- Rejected environmental determinism of Ratzel \& Semple
- Contributed in origin of agriculture, diffusion of plants and animals, impact of conquest on indigenous American societies
- Princeton Symposium (1955) — *Man's Role in Changing the Face of the Earth*
- **'Landscape'** — term introduced in 1925 with publication *The Morphology of Landscape*
- "The present 'cultural landscape' are to be studied in terms of their development from the original natural landscape"
- "Geography dissociates itself from geology at the point of introduction of man into the areal scene"
- Cultural Landscape consists of:
    - **General Geography** — study of form/elements (today's systematic geography)
    - **Regional Geography** — comparative morphology
    - **Historical Geography** — studies development in sequence or sequent occupance
- Aesthetic of landscape terms: Humboldt — *physiognomy*; Banse — *soul*; Volz — *rhythm*; Gradmann — *harmony*


### Ellsworth Huntington (1876–1947)

- Pupil of Davis; prolific writer; environmental determinist
- Tried to explain the style of life of human groups in the light of weather and climate
- Developed hypothesis about the outpouring of nomadic people from Central Asia → Mongol conquest
    - Book: *Pulse of Asia* (1907)
- **1915** — *Civilization and Climate* — civilizations could develop only in regions of stimulating weather
- *Principles of Human Geography* (1945)
- "Weather influences the energy of man"
- Gave too much weight to physical factors, especially climate


### Rollin D. Salisbury (1858–1922)

- Established Department of Geography at Chicago University
- *Physiography* — his book on evolution of landform
- Rejected the ideas of Semple


### New Concepts (Inter-war Period)

- **Human Ecology** — Barrows: adjustment of man to his natural environment
- **Chorology** — Mark Jefferson and Carl O. Sauer: to describe visible characteristics of an area; landscape has organic quality
- **Historical Geography** — laid down by Mackinder

## Soviet School of Geography

- Humboldt's geographical information guided eastward expansion of the Russian Empire
- **Imperial Geographical Society of Russia — 1845** — to promote geology, meteorology, hydrology, anthropology, and archaeology


### Peter Kropotkin (1842–1921)

- Founding thinker of anarchism
- Contributed to physical geography of northern Europe and Siberia
- Stressed role of co-operation and mutual aid in both organic evolution and peasant society
- Strongly opposed Darwin's *Origin of Species*; attacked Social Darwinism
- Influenced by Herbert Spencer
- In *general law of evolution*: all evolution is characterized by concentration, differentiation and determination
- Both Kropotkin and others stressed geography should encompass both humanity and nature


### V.V. Dokuchaiev (1846–1903)

- First professor of Geography in Russia
- Pioneering work in field of **geography soil**
- Different soils identified by layers of horizons — soil formation processes
- "Soil reflects the extraordinarily complex interaction of climate, slope, plants and animals with the parent material derived from the underlying geological formations"


### Marx \& Lenin on Geography

- **Marx** — geography was the necessary foundation on which the design of a new kind of economy was to be based
- **Lenin** — geography was essentially an applied subject; geography helped identify rational regions for national economy construction
- **GOSPLAN** — The State Planning Commission (1921) — divided country into 21 regions for detailed study


### Philosophy of Soviet Geographers

#### Anuchin

- *Theoretical Problems of Geography* — investigated theoretical basis of geography
- Against the dichotomy of physical and economic geography
- Geography to be studied with regional approach to delineate terrestrial complexes
- Basic concept: to build socialist economy and society
- **Landscape** — a dynamic concept in which matter and energy are circulating, with seasonal changes of heat and water balance and biological productivity


#### Gerasimov

- Landscape science inadequate for purposes of constructive geography
- Geography manifests considerable interest in the concept of **terrestrial industrial landscape**

# Part 4: Conceptual \& Methodological Development

## Man-Environment Relationship

**Introduction → Darwin's View → Deterministic View → Possibilistic View → Probabilism → Social Determinism**

### Key Thinkers on Man-Environment

- **Booke** — "to understand the earth as the world of man"
- **Einstein** — "the environment is everything that is not me"
- **Kant** — inhabitants of Torrid Zone are lazy and timid; Mediterranean region people are industrious and progressive
- **Humboldt** — mode of life differs between mountainous countries (Andes) and Amazon Basin
- **Ritter** — attempted to establish cause variations in physical constitution of body, physique and health in different physical environments


### Darwinism's Impact

- Before Darwin, man-environment relation was discussed as cause-effect relationship
- **Ratzel** — in *Anthropogeographie* (1882) — took the issue first time
- **Davis** — tried to incorporate causality into the definition of geography
- **Fleure** — man-environmental relationship should be studied from the physical point of view
- **Taylor** — race, population, states and cities are directly influenced by environmental factors
- **Hartshorne** — the whole determinist-possibilist debate was unreal and futile; led debate to philosophical rather than empirical level

***

## Determinism

**Platt (1948):** "Determinism refers to the idea that everything in human life is caused certainly by preceding events or condition."

- Due to difference in natural environment, variations in human behavior in different parts of the world can be described
- Level of development of history, culture, lifestyle and social groups is solely governed by physical components of environment
- Considers humans as **passive agents** on whom physical factors work continuously


### Historical Development

| Scholar | Argument |
| :-- | :-- |
| Aristotle | People in Asia were wise but lack of soul, thus subject to slavery; torrid and frigid zones uninhabitable |
| Strabo | Cold weather in Europe was the reason for their bravery |
| Montesquieu | People in cold weather are more courageous, less cunning; people in hot weather are weak, dull, inactive |
| Carl Ritter | Anti-human approach; laid foundation of determinism in geography |
| Humboldt | Life of hill country residents differs from those in plains |
| Ratzel | Supplemented classical determinism with Social Darwinism; developed state theory as organism |
| Semple | "Man is a product of the surface of the Earth" |
| Huntington (1945) | Climate and civilization; man's preference for ethnic-type structures and environmental exponents |
| Taylor | Environment has set the limit of human development |

**Black (1986):** "Environmental determinism was at the center of one of the longest debates in the history of the social science of Geography."

### Criticisms of Determinism

- **Barrows (1923)** — relationship between man and environment should be seen from standpoint of human adjustment
- **Sauer** — "Natural law does not apply to social groups"
- **Peet (1985)** — cultural geography of Blache and Sauer failed to establish a comprehensive theory
- **Hartshorne** — geography must analyse relationships of earth features regardless of whether they can be described in terms of natural laws or social laws


### Why Determinism Persisted (Conclusion)

1. Alternatives to determinism (environmentalism, possibilism, probabilism, cultural ecology, chorology) were less than satisfactory
2. Determinism was widely accepted as an understandable way of explaining complex social-cultural phenomena
3. Allied fields of knowledge were still following deterministic paths
4. No one ever stated that humans did not have the ability to choose from alternatives
5. Nor was there any argument that no other factors were at play in the development of human societies

***

## Possibilism

"It argues that the relationship between man and nature is not fixed — human beings have the capacity to choose between a range of possible responses to physical conditions."

### Historical Background

- Roots in the 2nd volume of *Anthropogeographie* by Ratzel
- Later inspiration for Vidal-de-la-Blache
- Direct contrast to determinism — places man in the first place; man as an **active agent**


### Key Thinkers

| Scholar | Contribution |
| :-- | :-- |
| Plato | Master of deductive reasoning — roots of possibilism |
| Montesquieu | Man possesses free will and ability to choose from series of opportunities; doctrine analogous to modern possibilism |
| Comte-de-Buffon | Man was ordered to conquer and even transform the earth |
| Vidal-de-la-Blache | "Nature has set boundaries and provided possibilities; response depends on traditional way of life"; *Genre de vie* |
| Lucien Febvre (1932) | "There are no necessities, but everywhere possibilities and man as the master of these possibilities, is the judge of their use" |
| Brunhes (1910) | *La Géographie de L'histoire* — disseminated possibilism; "nature is not compulsory but the approval" |
| Barrows | Geography should be studied from man's adjustment to environment |
| Hettner | Both physical and human factors are to be studied in their relationship to each other |

### Genre de Vie (Vidal-de-la-Blache)

- "Way of life"
- Includes all activities, practices and techniques characterizing the adaptation of human group to the milieu
- 1913 — Geography as a discipline seeks to measure and role of man in modifying the earth surface


### Criticism of Possibilism

- **Griffith Taylor** — society should elect entirely; geography's work is not of explanatory nature
- Leaves unsolved the question: **why one possibility rather than another?**
- Was the choice free or necessitated by some other factor — psychological, political or economic?

***

## Probabilism

**O.H.K. Spate (1957):**

- "The physical environment does not specifically determine human activities yet it gives some reaction to others"
- "Human action was not said to be in the case of all on some kind of compulsion; but rather the balance of the possibilities"
- After this, probability theory considered an essential component of geographic analysis


### Cultural / Social Darwinism

- **Edward Wellman** — "The environment is essentially natural; its role depends on the level of technology, the types of culture and other characteristics of changing society"
- **George Carter** — gave more emphasis on cultural forces — "ideas that determine the human use of the physical world"

***

## Quantitative Revolution

- Came into account in **1950s and 1960s** — replaced the ideographic approach; based on **nomothetic approach**
- Laid basis of geography as a **spatial science** dealing with spatial analysis of phenomena on the earth surface
- Gave geography a scientific vision through statistical methodology

**Burton:** "This school had set out to discover universals, to build models and to establish methods and theoretical base on which geographical realities could be created."

### Main Objectives

1. To change the narrative character of the subject (geo + physics) and make it a scientific discipline
2. To explain and interpret the spatial patterns of geographic phenomena in a logical and objective way
3. Deals with the use of mathematical and statistical techniques
4. To make accurate statements about location order
5. To prepare estimates, principles and laws for testing estimates and forecasts
6. To provide a sound philosophical and theoretical base to geography and make it a scientific discipline

### Dichotomies Introduced

1. Measurement by instruments vs. direct sensed data
2. Rational analysis vs. intuitive perception
3. Cold scientific constructs vs. rich daily sensed experience from the real world
4. Constantly changing phenomena vs. discrete cases
5. **Nomothetic vs. Idiographic**
